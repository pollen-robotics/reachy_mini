# Teaching Reachy Mini to Wobble Its Head in Sync With Speech

A working journal of how we go from "robot wobbles continuously while talking" to "robot's head moves *with* the syllables." Each section is added as we iterate. Plots are regenerated by `python -m examples.wobbler_lab.run` and live under `out/`.

## 1. The problem

Reachy Mini's "head wobbler" reacts to speech audio in real time and produces 6-DOF head offsets (pitch, yaw, roll, plus x/y/z translation). Four versions exist:

| version | idea |
|---|---|
| **v0** | VAD-gated envelope drives 6 independent sine oscillators. |
| v1 | No VAD; RMS energy directly scales the same oscillators. |
| v2 | Multi-band energy: low → pitch, mid → yaw, high → roll. |
| v3 | RMS-onset detector spawns decaying impulses, plus background sway. |

Subjective verdict from running them on Reachy Mini: only **v0** looks acceptable, and only on speech with clear pauses between words. As soon as the speaker runs continuous syllables, every version becomes "dumb sinus wobbling that has no reason to be synchronized with the intonations."

Why? Because they are all **continuous oscillators modulated by an envelope**. The envelope changes slowly; the speech inside it changes fast. So the head swings at oscillator frequencies (0.25 to 2.2 Hz), and those swings have nothing to do with where syllables actually land.

**Going forward**: v0 is the immutable baseline. v1, v2, v3 are frozen as historical experiments and won't be iterated. New ideas land as v4, v5, etc.

## 2. The lab: running the wobbler offline

To iterate without the robot we built a tiny harness in `examples/wobbler_lab/`:

| file | purpose |
|---|---|
| `simulate.py` | Loads a WAV, runs any `speech_tapper_*` against it in 1 s chunks, returns a `(T, 6)` motion matrix at the 50 ms hop grid. |
| `features.py` | Computes RMS dB, onset strength, voicing and F0 (`librosa.pyin`), and syllable nuclei (peaks of voiced RMS, min spacing 150 ms). |
| `metrics.py` | Three sync scores (see §4). |
| `plot.py` | Stacked figure: spectrogram, RMS, F0, onset envelope, and 6 motion axes, all aligned on the same time axis. |
| `run.py` | CLI driver. Writes plots to `out/`, prints a metric table. |

It uses the same three TTS clips from `sound_tts.py` (Kennedy, MLK, De Gaulle) so we always look at the same material across versions.

## 3. What "synchronized" actually means

Listening to good talking-head animation systems, three things tend to be true:

1. **Silence is silent.** The head should be *still* when nothing is being said. This is the cheap win, and the user's hardest requirement: "silences should be silences."
2. **Motion energy concentrates on voiced regions.** Vowels and voiced consonants drive movement; fricatives and pauses don't.
3. **Motion peaks land on syllable nuclei or speech onsets.** A nod or a sway should kick at the start of a stressed syllable, not at a random oscillator phase.

These three give us the three metrics in `metrics.py`.

## 4. Three metrics

Computed per-clip × per-version on the 50 ms hop grid:

* **`stillness_in_silence`** is `1 - mean|m|_silence / mean|m|_overall`, where silence is RMS below -55 dBFS. Higher is better (≈ 1 means motion goes to zero in pauses; ≈ 0 means motion is the same in silence as in speech).
* **`voiced_unvoiced_ratio`** is `mean|m|_voiced / mean|m|_unvoiced`. Higher is better. Above 1 means voiced frames carry more motion than unvoiced ones.
* **`onset_alignment`**: for each detected onset, compute `max |Δm|` in a 150 ms window starting at the onset. Compare to 25 random time-shifts of the same onset set. A score above 1 means motion peaks really do cluster on onsets; ≈ 1 means no better than chance.

The first two reward the silence gate; the third asks "are you actually syncing to the speech, or just being on?"

## 6. Diagnosis

1. **The VAD gate is the only feature in v0 that still works.** Drop it (v1, v2, v3) and stillness-in-silence collapses from ≈ 1.0 to between 0.49 and 0.69.
2. **None of the four are syllable-aware.** The best onset-alignment score (1.015 on De Gaulle v2) is statistical noise; all four hover around 1.0 ± chance.
3. **v3 had the right intuition** (impulses on events) **but the wrong detector**. RMS deltas inside a voiced run fire constantly. The right trigger is *peaks of voiced RMS with a minimum inter-peak spacing*, i.e. actual syllable nuclei.
4. **The user reports v0's VAD sometimes fails to deactivate**, leaving the head wobbling through pauses. The release rule (RMS below -45 dBFS for 250 ms) is too loose against reverb tails, breaths, and room tone. New versions must combine RMS with a voicing check.

## 7. Plan for v4

Three changes from v0/v3:

1. **Stricter silence gate**: combine the RMS threshold with a streaming autocorrelation-based voicing detector. Force motion to zero as soon as voicing drops AND RMS is below a moderate floor for ~100 ms. This addresses the "doesn't deactivate" complaint directly.
2. **Syllable-nucleus direction tracking** instead of sine oscillators. Each detected nucleus picks a new random 6-axis direction; the head smoothly lerps from the previous direction to the new one. Long held vowels keep the head moving in a coherent direction (no sudden freeze between syllables); a new syllable causes a smooth direction change. No oscillators, no impulse-decay, just one moving target.
3. **Amplitude follows voiced-energy envelope**, hard-gated by the strict VAD. So volume controls how big the gestures are; voicing controls whether anything happens at all.

Optional v5 if v4 lands well: add an F0-derivative term that nudges head pitch up on rising intonation and down on falling intonation.

## 8. v4 first run

v4 is implemented in `src/reachy_mini/motion/speech_tapper_v4.py`. The two key blocks:

**Strict VAD.** Each hop, we compute RMS dB and a streaming voicing flag (autocorrelation peak in the 80 to 400 Hz lag range). VAD is active iff RMS is above a moderate floor (-50 dBFS) AND either RMS recently exceeded -32 dBFS, OR a voiced frame was seen in the last 120 ms. The "OR voicing" branch lets normal speech (which is voiced but not always loud) keep the wobbler active without lowering the loud threshold; the "AND moderate floor" branch hard-mutes on reverb tails and breath.

**Direction tracking.** A per-hop nucleus detector watches a streaming "voiced loudness" signal (loudness gain when voiced, zero otherwise) and fires when the previous hop was a local peak with prominence above 0.10 (on a [0, 1] scale) and at least 150 ms since the last nucleus. On each detection it samples a new sharpened-random 6-axis direction (one or two axes dominate) and stores it as `target_dir`. Each hop, the head direction `current_dir` lerps toward `target_dir` (coefficient 0.18, time constant ~280 ms), and `target_dir` itself decays back toward zero (factor 0.92 per hop). The final motion is `current_dir * envelope * per_axis_amplitude`.

### Tuning, three iterations

The first run used `DIR_LERP = 0.18`, `DIR_DECAY = 0.92`, `NUCLEUS_PROMINENCE = 0.10`. Stillness was already perfect on every clip, but Kennedy's onset alignment regressed badly (0.84, below chance).

|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| baseline v0 | (n/a) | (n/a) | (n/a) | 1.013 | 0.997 | 0.968 |
| v4 #1 | 0.18 | 0.92 | 0.10 | 1.118 | **0.839** | 1.069 |

| **v4 #4 (locked)** | **0.45** | **0.82** | **0.10** | **1.231** | **1.033** | **1.364** |

What changed: lowering `NUCLEUS_PROMINENCE` made things worse (Kennedy 0.84 → 0.80). The right move was to make the *response* faster, not detect more events. Raising `DIR_LERP` from 0.18 to 0.45 means the velocity transient after each nucleus completes within ~110 ms instead of ~280 ms, which fits inside the 150 ms onset window. Sharpening `DIR_DECAY` from 0.92 to 0.82 amplifies the transient (target relaxes faster between nuclei, so each new target has farther to travel from `current_dir`).

## 9. v5: F0-relative pitch tilt on top of v4

v5 keeps everything from v4 (strict gate, nucleus-driven yaw/roll/x/y/z) and only changes the pitch axis. New behaviour: the head pitch gets a small extra tilt proportional to how far the current intonation sits above or below a *running F0 baseline*. Slow self-adaptation in the baseline (16 s EMA) makes this work for any speaker pitch range without manual calibration.

### Streaming F0 estimation

Voicing detection in v4 already runs an autocorrelation on each 40 ms frame and keeps only "is the peak above threshold" as a boolean. v5 reuses the same FFT-based autocorrelation and additionally returns the *lag of the peak*, with parabolic interpolation around it for sub-sample accuracy. Lag → period → F0 in Hz, no extra DSP cost.

### From F0 to head pitch

Per hop, when voiced and VAD active:

1. Smooth raw F0 into `f0_smoothed` (alpha 0.4, ~150 ms time constant).
2. Convert to semitones above 100 Hz: `f0_st = 12 * log2(f0_smoothed / 100)`.
3. Update the running baseline `f0_baseline_st` with a slow EMA (alpha 0.003, ~16 s time constant). Initialized to the first observed `f0_st` so it doesn't have to drift up from zero.
4. `tilt_st = f0_st - f0_baseline_st`. Positive when current intonation is above the speaker's recent average, negative when below.
5. `tilt_deg = clamp(tilt_st * 0.6, ±4°)`. Multiplied by the loudness envelope so quiet voiced frames don't tilt as much as loud ones.

The final pitch axis output is `v4_pitch + tilt_current`, where `tilt_current` is a smoothed version of `tilt_target` (TILT_LERP = 0.3). When VAD goes off, tilt is hard-zeroed so silences stay silent on the pitch axis too.

## 10. Robot session 1: too small, jerky, then noticeably late

The first physical-robot run with the v4 numbers from §8 surfaced three problems the offline metrics didn't catch:

1. **Movements were "way too small"** to even tell whether sync was working.
2. After bumping amplitudes 2x, motion was bigger but **jerky**, with each gesture spike-and-returning.
3. After smoothing the lerp/decay so each gesture stepped-and-held, motion was smoother but **felt like the head froze between syllables**, and **lagged the audio at the start of each phrase** (catching up only by the end of sentences).

A fourth observation isolated the most important issue: **the second TTS clip (a quieter female voice) produced significantly smaller motion than the first** (a louder male voice). Same algorithm, same amplitudes, very different feel depending on input level.

That last point named the problem precisely. Every threshold in v4 (the loudness gain, the nucleus prominence, the rise threshold) was an *absolute* dB number. Quieter speech walked into the algorithm with smaller `_loudness_gain(db)` values and produced smaller motion. Louder speech got larger gains and larger motion.

## 11. v4 with AGC + rising-edge triggers

Two fixes for the issues from the robot session:

### Speaker-relative AGC

A slow EMA tracks `speech_db`, the running average dB level over voiced+loud frames (alpha 0.008, ~6 s time constant; initialized to -25 dBFS so the first speech still moves). The loudness gain function becomes speaker-relative: rather than mapping absolute dB to motion, it maps `db - speech_db` to motion through a small relative window (-8 dB to +6 dB around the speaker's mean). The same window applies to a quiet voice and a loud voice; both produce the full dynamic range of motion.

A hard absolute floor (`db < -50` forces gain to 0) preserves the strict-silence guarantee regardless of the AGC state.

### Rising-edge nucleus triggers

The previous version detected nuclei as *peaks of voiced loudness* with a 1-hop lookback ("was the previous frame a local max?"). Correct in principle, but lagged the audio by the syllable's rise time (~50-150 ms from onset to peak) plus the 50 ms lookback, plus the lerp time constant. Total perceived latency at the start of a phrase: ~225 ms.

The new detector is a rising-edge trigger: the trigger fires the first hop where `loud_voiced` is greater than the rolling minimum over the last 3 hops by more than `NUCLEUS_RISE_THRESHOLD`. There is no lookback. Triggers fire ~50-100 ms earlier than peak detection, so the head starts moving closer to syllable onset rather than after the syllable's loudness peak.

Both `loud_voiced` and the rolling minimum are computed on the AGC-normalized gain, so the trigger threshold also adapts to speaker volume.

### Where the wobbler runs (and why this matters)

`HeadWobbler` is instantiated in `src/reachy_mini/media/audio_gstreamer.py`, inside the daemon. On Lite robots that's the user's laptop; on Wireless robots that's the on-board CM4 (Pi 4, 1.5 GHz quad ARM). One 1280-point real FFT per 50 ms hop is about 50 µs on a CM4, so the live wobbler is well under 1 % CPU on either platform. None of the lag is due to compute pressure; it was all algorithmic.

## 12. Robot session 2: AGC normalized speakers, but the head still froze between syllables

The AGC + rising-edge tuning fixed the volume sensitivity (quieter MLK now produced motion comparable to louder Kennedy) and shaved noticeable lag at phrase starts. But two things were still off:

1. **Too many moments where the head stops moving** while the speaker is still talking. The discrete nucleus model leaves dead air between triggers, especially during sustained voicing without clear loudness peaks.
2. **6 s AGC adaptation is too cautious** for this pipeline. Speech here arrives directly through TTS, no mic noise, no DJ ramp; the speaker's mean level is essentially knowable in the first second.

User insight worth recording: v0/v1's continuous sine *did* have one strength worth keeping. Even when the wave was disconnected from the actual speech, the brain happily associated the two. Pure discrete-gesture motion lacks that fallback.

## 13. Three more changes for v4

### 1. AGC ~5x faster

```
SPEECH_LEVEL_ALPHA: 0.008 → 0.040  (~6 s → ~1.25 s time constant)
SPEECH_DEFAULT_DB:   -25  → -28    (closer to typical TTS speech mean)
```

Speech in this pipe is high-quality and consistent. The slow tracker was protecting against false adaptation that doesn't happen here.

### 2. More frequent triggers

```
NUCLEUS_RISE_THRESHOLD: 0.15 → 0.08
NUCLEUS_MIN_GAIN:        0.30 → 0.18
```

The rising-edge detector now catches subtler variations within voiced spans. Each one gets a target direction change and a short follow-through gesture. Combined with the breath layer below, dead air shrinks substantially.

### 3. Continuous "breath" layer

Six small sines (one per axis), envelope-gated so they vanish in silence, with frequencies in the 0.3-1.5 Hz range and amplitudes about 25-30 % of the nucleus-driven peaks. Pitch 1.5° at 1.5 Hz, yaw 3.0° at 0.6 Hz, roll 1.0° at 1.0 Hz, translations 0.8-1.5 mm at 0.3-0.45 Hz.

```python
breath_yaw = math.radians(BREATH_A_YAW_DEG) * env * sin(2π * BREATH_F_YAW * t + phase_yaw)
yaw = math.radians(A_YAW_DEG) * d[1] * env + breath_yaw
```

This gives the head an always-on undercurrent during voicing (the part of v0 worth keeping) without dominating the discrete syllable-driven gestures (which are still the perceived "main motion"). Crucially `env` goes to zero in silence so the breath disappears too: the strict-silence guarantee survives.

## 14. The lateness was structural, not compute

After §13, the motion was decent but the head still felt like it was reacting half a beat after the audio. Worth a careful walkthrough of where the lag actually comes from, because the same fix has to work for two completely different ways audio reaches the wobbler.

### The two audio paths

The wobbler doesn't talk directly to the audio device. It receives raw audio samples from a media pipeline built on GStreamer (a Linux/Mac multimedia framework). The pipeline is in `src/reachy_mini/media/audio_gstreamer.py` and there are two flavours of it:

| Path | Source of audio | Used by |
|---|---|---|
| **File** | A GStreamer playbin reads a WAV/MP3 file, decodes it, hands buffers to a tee element. | `mini.media.play_sound("file.wav")`, which is what `examples/sound_tts.py` uses. |
| **Live** | A Python-fed appsrc element where `push_audio_sample(pcm)` writes raw float32 samples directly. | LLM agents, conversation apps, any code that produces audio at runtime. |

Both flavours share the same downstream pipeline: a tee splits the stream into two branches. One branch goes to the speaker (`audiosink`). The other branch goes to a Python callback (`appsink`). The callback `_on_wobbler_sample` (line 268 of `audio_gstreamer.py`) is what hands raw samples to the head wobbler.

So the wobbler doesn't care whether the audio came from a file or from `push_audio_sample`. Any timing fix has to apply to both. That is the entire constraint.

### The "play at" timestamp

Each time the wobbler callback fires, it computes the wall-clock instant at which the *first sample* of that audio buffer will physically come out of the speaker. The variable is called `play_at_ns` (`ns` for nanoseconds, `time.monotonic_ns()` is the system's monotonic wall clock in nanoseconds). It is computed two different ways depending on a GStreamer property called `sync` on the appsink:

* **`sync=True`**: GStreamer holds the appsink callback until the buffer's intended playout time, then fires it. The audio is being played *now*. So `play_at_ns = time.monotonic_ns()`. The wobbler has zero lookahead.
* **`sync=False`**: The callback fires as soon as the buffer arrives at the appsink, which is *before* the speaker plays it. The audiosink has its own internal buffer of `PLAYBACK_SINK_BUFFER_TIME_US` microseconds (set at the top of `audio_gstreamer.py`, currently 50 000, i.e. 50 ms). So the audio plays 50 ms after the callback fires, and `play_at_ns = time.monotonic_ns() + 50 ms`. The wobbler gets 50 ms of lookahead.

Until now, the file path used `sync=True` (zero lookahead) and the live path used `sync=False` (50 ms lookahead). Asymmetric.

### What HeadWobbler does with `play_at_ns`

In `src/reachy_mini/motion/head_wobbler.py`, `HeadWobbler.feed(pcm, play_at_monotonic_ns)` does three things:

1. Calls `SwayRollRT.feed(pcm)` to turn the raw samples into a list of motion offsets, one per 50 ms hop.
2. For each hop `i`, computes `target_ns = play_at_monotonic_ns + i * hop_ns`. That is the wall-clock instant at which the audio sample at offset `i * hop_size` will play.
3. Schedules a callback to fire at `target_ns` (using `GLib.timeout_add`, which is a main-loop timer). When the callback fires, it writes the offset to the head pose, and the SDK pushes it down to the motors.

So scheduling is exact. Hop `i` of motion fires at the same wall-clock instant that audio sample `i * hop_size` comes out of the speaker. There is no scheduling drift.

### Where the perceived lag actually came from

Three components, none of them compute time:

1. **Analysis frame is the recent past.** When the speech tapper is asked to produce a motion offset for time `t`, it looks at the last 40 ms of audio ending at `t`. So the motion at `t` reflects audio that played up to `t`, not audio that's about to play. ~20-40 ms of inherent lag.

2. **The smoothing inside the speech tapper.** When a syllable triggers, the variable `target_dir` jumps to a new value. The variable `current_dir` (which is what becomes the actual motor command) lerps toward `target_dir` with `DIR_LERP = 0.40` per hop. It takes ~3 hops (150 ms) for `current_dir` to reach 63 % of the new target, and another ~3 hops to reach 95 %. So when the audio peaks at sample `N`, the motion peaks at sample `N + ~150 ms`. **This is the dominant source of perceived lateness.**

3. **Rising-edge detection latency.** Used to be 50 ms with the peak-and-look-back design from §8. The §11 rewrite removed it.

The smoothing in (2) is intentional: without it, every syllable would cause an abrupt jump and the motion would look jerky on the robot. So we cannot just make `DIR_LERP` larger.

### The fix: schedule motion earlier than the audio

Two small code changes:

**1. Symmetrise the file and live paths** (`src/reachy_mini/media/audio_gstreamer.py`, line 314): switch the file-path appsink to `sync=False`. Now both paths get 50 ms of lookahead, set by `PLAYBACK_SINK_BUFFER_TIME_US`.

**2. Schedule motion `LATENCY_COMPENSATION_NS` earlier than the corresponding audio sample** (`src/reachy_mini/motion/head_wobbler.py`):

```python
target_ns = play_at_monotonic_ns + i * hop_ns - LATENCY_COMPENSATION_NS
```

With `LATENCY_COMPENSATION_NS = 50 ms`, motion at hop `i` is scheduled to fire 50 ms before audio sample `i * hop_size` plays. The smoothing then has 50 ms to ramp before the audio arrives at the listener's ear. Compensation is bounded by lookahead: anything more than the audiosink buffer (50 ms) would schedule motion in the past for samples already committed to the sink, and the head wobbler would skip those hops (`if delay_ms < stale_threshold_ms: continue` at line 140).

`LATENCY_COMPENSATION_MS` is overridable at runtime via the environment variable `WOBBLER_LATENCY_COMPENSATION_MS`, default 50. To go beyond 50 ms compensation, we'd also have to raise `PLAYBACK_SINK_BUFFER_TIME_US` to give the appsink more lookahead. The cost of a bigger sink buffer is a slightly slower reaction at the very *start* of speech (the speaker takes that much longer to produce the first sound after `push_audio_sample` is called); during continuous speech everything stays in sync.

### What this preserves

* Both `play_sound(file)` and `push_audio_sample(pcm)` get the same fix because both go through the same callback in the same pipeline.
* The strict-silence guarantee is unchanged (the speech tapper still gates motion to zero in silence).
* The metrics are unchanged (the lab harness simulates the speech tapper directly, not the GStreamer pipeline).
* Default behaviour matches the old behaviour if `WOBBLER_LATENCY_COMPENSATION_MS=0` is set in the environment.

## 15. Robot session 3: the file-path fix was wrong, here's why

The change in §14 made the head move a lot at the start of each clip and then go dead, plus felt more delayed overall. Diagnosed root cause: the file path in `_build_audiosink_tee_bin` is fed by a `playbin` whose file decoder runs much faster than real time. With `sync=True` on the wobbler appsink (original), GStreamer holds each buffer until its presentation timestamp matches the pipeline clock, so the wobbler callback fires at real-time intervals. With `sync=False` (the change), the appsink consumes buffers as fast as they arrive: tens or hundreds of callbacks fire within the first ~100 ms of playback while the decoder dumps the whole clip into the queue, each call setting `play_at_ns = time.monotonic_ns() + 50 ms` regardless of which chunk it actually corresponds to. The head wobbler then schedules a tight cluster of motion at the start, leaving the rest of the clip's audio with no scheduled motion. The original docstring on `_make_wobbler_appsink` even spelled this out: *"sync=True for playbin-based paths so new-sample fires at the buffer's PTS on the pipeline clock"*. I missed it.

**Reverted** the `sync=False` change in `_build_audiosink_tee_bin`. The file path is back to its original behaviour.

`LATENCY_COMPENSATION_MS` stays in `head_wobbler.py`, but it now only meaningfully affects the live path (`push_audio_sample`), where `sync=False` is correct because the source is a live appsrc that paces its own output. On the file path the compensation hits the negative-delay clamp (`max(0, delay_ms)`) at line 150 of `head_wobbler.py` and effectively becomes a no-op, which is the same behaviour as before. So the file path is unchanged from §13 and the live path will get up to 50 ms of head start.

### Where the lookahead has to come from for the file path

To make the file path actually compensate latency, we need lookahead that respects the audio playout timeline (not just "now plus a constant"). Two viable approaches for a future iteration:

1. **Use the buffer's presentation timestamp.** Each buffer arriving at the wobbler appsink has a `pts` attribute set by GStreamer relative to the pipeline clock. Combined with the pipeline's clock and base time, the callback can compute the wall-clock instant at which this specific buffer's audio will play, regardless of when the callback fires. With this, `sync=False` on the file path becomes safe: the burst of callbacks at decode time would each get a different `play_at_ns` aligned to its actual playout time. Motion is scheduled correctly, and the burst-decoded buffers stop being a problem.

2. **Add an `audiodelay` element on the speaker branch only.** Keep `sync=True` on the wobbler appsink. Insert a fixed delay (say 100 ms) between `audioresample` and `audiosink` on the speaker branch. Now the appsink callback fires at the buffer's intended playout time, but the speaker plays the audio 100 ms later. The head wobbler can subtract that 100 ms from `target_ns` and the motion is genuinely 100 ms ahead of audio. The cost is 100 ms of added latency at the start of every clip, which for `sound_tts.py` is unnoticeable.

Approach 2 is simpler. Approach 1 is more general (it would also let live audio go beyond the current 50 ms compensation cap by enlarging the audiosink buffer, which Approach 2 already requires anyway). Either could be tried next session if 50 ms on the live path isn't enough.
